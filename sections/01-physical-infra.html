Layer 0

Physical Infrastructure

The foundation of GPU-accelerated HPC: facility design, power delivery, thermal management, and floor planning for modern AI/ML datacenters.

1. Datacenter Facility

GPU superpod deployments demand facilities engineered far beyond traditional enterprise datacenters. A single NVIDIA DGX SuperPOD with 32 DGX H100 nodes draws approximately 330 kW of IT power alone — before cooling overhead. At scale, a facility hosting thousands of GPUs requires 20–50+ MW of total capacity, robust redundancy, and strict physical security.

Typical Facility Specifications

+

Total Capacity

20–50+ MW per facility; individual halls typically provisioned at 2–8 MW each. A 40 MW campus can support roughly 4,000–6,000 H100 GPUs depending on cooling overhead and PUE.

Whitespace

50,000–200,000+ sq ft of raised-floor or slab data hall space. GPU-dense halls often allocate 80–120 sq ft per rack (including hot-aisle containment and CDU footprint).

Network Meet-Me Rooms

Carrier-neutral MMRs with diverse fiber entry points. Minimum two physically separate fiber paths from different conduit routes. Cross-connects to upstream transit and peering providers.

Staging & Storage

Dedicated build rooms for rack assembly and burn-in testing before deployment to the data hall. Spare parts inventory for GPUs, NICs, NVMe drives, fans, PSUs, and coolant hoses.

2. Power Distribution

Power architecture for GPU-dense facilities is fundamentally different from traditional datacenters. A single 42U rack of 4× DGX H100 systems can draw 40–100+ kW — an order of magnitude above legacy 5–10 kW server racks. Every element of the power chain must be designed for these loads with full redundancy.

Utility Feed & Medium Voltage Switchgear

+

Dual Utility Feeds

Critical GPU facilities receive power from two independent utility substations fed by separate transmission lines. This eliminates single-point-of-failure at the utility level. Each feed is typically rated at 100% of the facility's total load, providing true 2N utility redundancy.

Common configurations: 2×35 kV or 2×13.8 kV feeds, depending on local utility infrastructure. Facilities >30 MW often take delivery at 69 kV or 138 kV and step down on-site via customer-owned substations.

Medium Voltage Switchgear

Incoming utility at 13.8 kV (or 34.5 kV) is routed through metal-clad, arc-resistant switchgear rated to ANSI C37.20.2. Vacuum circuit breakers provide fault isolation with interrupting capacity typically rated at 25–40 kA.

Step-down transformers (13.8 kV → 480V, dry-type or liquid-filled) serve the mechanical and IT loads. For GPU racks, 480V is further stepped to 208V three-phase (via 480:208/120V Delta-Wye PDU transformers) or delivered at 240V single-phase in some configurations.

Diesel Generators

+

Startup & Transfer

Modern standby generators (e.g., Caterpillar 3516, MTU 4000 series) are rated for startup to full load in 10–15 seconds. Block heaters keep coolant at 40–50°C for reliable cold-start. The Automatic Transfer Switch (ATS) detects utility loss (voltage sag below 80% for >100 ms) and sends a start signal. Once the generator reaches rated voltage and frequency (±3%), the ATS transfers load. Total utility-to-generator transfer: 15–25 seconds.

Fuel & Runtime

On-site fuel storage: typically 24–72 hours at full load. A 2.5 MW generator consumes roughly 700 liters/hour of diesel at full load. Priority fuel contracts with multiple suppliers guarantee resupply within 4–8 hours. Base fuel tanks (belly tanks) hold 2,000–4,000 liters; supplemental day tanks or bulk storage extend runtime.

Load Testing

Monthly: 30-minute loaded test at ≥30% rated load (prevents wet-stacking). Annually: 4-hour full-load test per NFPA 110 Level 1 requirements. Resistive load banks are used when IT load is insufficient for proper testing. All tests logged for compliance audits.

Emissions & Permitting

EPA Tier 4 Final emissions standards for new installations. Selective Catalytic Reduction (SCR) or Diesel Particulate Filters (DPF) required. Operating permits limit annual run hours (typically 200–500 hours for standby-rated units). Facilities exceeding limits must apply for prime-rated permits.

Voltage & Phase Configurations

208V Three-Phase (Wye)

Most common in North American datacenters. Derived from 480V→208Y/120V transformer. Three hot legs + neutral. 208V line-to-line, 120V line-to-neutral. Typical PDU: 3-phase 60A (208V) = ~21.6 kVA per PDU. For a 80 kW GPU rack, two 3-phase 60A PDUs on separate power paths (A+B feeds).

240V Single-Phase

Common in some US facilities and international deployments. Higher voltage = lower current for the same power, allowing smaller conductors. A single 240V/50A circuit delivers ~12 kVA. Less common for high-density GPU racks due to limited per-circuit capacity.

400V Three-Phase (European/Hyperscale)

Growing adoption even in US hyperscale facilities. 400V three-phase eliminates one transformer stage (direct from 480V with minor ratio adjustment), improving efficiency by 1–2%. Google, Microsoft, and Meta have adopted 400V distribution for new builds.

SRE Tip: Always verify PDU firmware supports SNMP v3 (encrypted) and HTTPS. Older PDUs with SNMP v1/v2c expose community strings in plaintext. Integrate PDU telemetry into your DCIM platform (e.g., Sunbird, Nlyte, or custom Prometheus exporters) for real-time power monitoring and alerting at ≥80% circuit capacity.

Power Redundancy Models

+
Model Description Effective Capacity Cost
N No redundancy. Single power path. Any component failure causes outage. 100% of installed capacity usable Baseline
N+1 One additional component beyond minimum required. E.g., if 4 UPS modules needed, install 5. One can fail or be maintained without impact. 80–85% of installed capacity usable +20–25%
2N Fully duplicated power path. Two independent chains (utility → ATS → UPS → PDU), each sized for 100% load. Servers with dual PSUs draw from both paths. 50% of total installed capacity usable +80–100%
2N+1 Two complete paths plus one additional redundant component per path. Maximum resilience. ~48% of total installed capacity usable +100–120%
GPU Rack Consideration: DGX H100 systems have dual redundant 3 kW PSUs (6 PSUs total per node, operating in 3+3 redundancy). Each set must be connected to a separate power path (A feed and B feed). If both PDUs are on the same electrical path, you have N redundancy, not 2N, even though the hardware supports it. Verify all the way back to the UPS.

PUE & Power Density

+

Power Usage Effectiveness (PUE)

PUE = Total Facility Power ÷ IT Equipment Power

Industry average: ~1.58 (Uptime Institute 2023 survey). Best-in-class air-cooled: 1.2–1.3. Liquid-cooled GPU facilities: 1.1–1.2 achievable. Every 0.1 PUE improvement on a 40 MW facility saves ~4 MW of overhead power, translating to $2–4M/year in electricity costs at $0.06/kWh.

Per-Rack Power Density

Traditional enterprise: 5–10 kW/rack. GPU-accelerated HPC: 30–100+ kW/rack. A rack of 4× DGX H100 (8 GPUs each = 32 GPUs): ~40 kW IT + networking. A full OCP rack with 8× HGX H100 baseboard: ~80–100 kW. NVIDIA GB200 NVL72 rack: up to 120 kW.

3. Cooling

Cooling is the single biggest engineering challenge in GPU-dense datacenters. An NVIDIA DGX H100 node with 8× H100 SXM5 GPUs dissipates approximately 10.2 kW of thermal energy. A rack with 4 DGX H100 systems produces 40+ kW of heat in a 42U footprint — far exceeding the 5–7 kW/rack that traditional CRAC/CRAH air cooling was designed for. At 80–120 kW/rack densities (GB200 NVL72), air cooling is physically impossible; liquid cooling is mandatory.

Traditional Air Cooling & Its Limitations

+

Conventional Computer Room Air Handlers (CRAHs) circulate chilled air through raised-floor plenums or overhead ducts. Practical limits:

Metric Air Cooling Limit GPU Rack Requirement
Heat removal per rack 15–25 kW (with containment) 40–120 kW
Airflow per rack ~3,000–5,000 CFM achievable 8,000–15,000+ CFM needed
Fan energy 30–40% of cooling energy spent on fans Unacceptable PUE impact at scale
Noise at rack 70–80 dBA typical 90+ dBA with high-speed fans — hearing protection required
Why containment helps but isn’t enough: Hot aisle/cold aisle containment prevents mixing of supply and return air, improving CRAH efficiency by 20–30%. With containment, air cooling can handle up to ~25 kW/rack. Beyond that threshold, the volumetric airflow required simply cannot be delivered through standard raised-floor tiles (each perforated tile delivers ~500–800 CFM).

Liquid Cooling Technologies

+

Direct-to-Chip (Cold Plate) Cooling

Cold plates are machined copper or aluminum blocks mounted directly on CPUs and GPUs with thermal interface material (TIM). Facility water (or a water-glycol mix) flows through microchannels in the cold plate, absorbing heat via conduction.

How It Works

Each GPU module has a dedicated cold plate connected via flexible hoses to a manifold in the rack. The manifold connects to the facility Coolant Distribution Unit (CDU). Supply water enters at 30–45°C and exits at 45–60°C. The high delta-T allows the use of warmer water, enabling free cooling for more hours of the year.

Performance

Removes 70–80% of rack heat via liquid (the remaining 20–30% from DIMMs, NVMe drives, NICs, VRMs is still dissipated via fans into room air). Flow rate: typically 1.5–4.0 liters/minute per GPU cold plate. Pressure drop across a fully populated rack manifold: 30–80 kPa (4–12 PSI).

Leak Detection

Non-negotiable requirement. Conductive fluid leak sensors on every hose connection, under every rack, and at every CDU. Leak detection ropes (e.g., RLE Technologies SeaHawk) run the length of every liquid-cooled row. Automated shutoff valves isolate a leaking rack within seconds. All connections use dripless quick-disconnect fittings (e.g., Staubli or CPC) rated for thousands of connect/disconnect cycles with <1 mL fluid loss per event.

NVIDIA Implementation

The DGX H100 liquid-cooled variant uses a custom cold plate assembly covering all 8 H100 SXM5 GPUs and the dual CPUs. The DGX SuperPOD reference architecture specifies CoolIT DCLC or Vertiv XDU CDUs. The GB200 NVL72 rack is liquid-cooled only — no air-cooled option exists.

Rear-Door Heat Exchangers (RDHx)

A passive or active heat exchanger replaces the standard rear door of the rack. Chilled water flows through a copper coil in the door, absorbing heat from exhaust air before it enters the room. Can neutralize 60–100% of rack heat, effectively making the rack “zero-emission” to the room. Suitable for up to ~40 kW/rack. Not sufficient alone for high-density GPU racks above 50 kW — often combined with direct-to-chip cooling to handle the remaining airborne heat.

Full Immersion Cooling

IT equipment is submerged in a dielectric fluid (e.g., 3M Novec 7100 engineered fluid, or mineral/synthetic oil). Single-phase immersion keeps the fluid liquid (bath temperature ~35–50°C). Two-phase immersion uses a low-boiling-point fluid (boiling point ~49–61°C for Novec fluids) that vaporizes at the chip surface and condenses on an overhead coil, providing extremely efficient heat transfer.

Immersion can handle >100 kW/tank but requires custom server form factors (no fans, open chassis design, compatible connectors). Adoption is growing but still niche for GPU superpods due to serviceability challenges — you must extract servers from fluid to replace components.

CDUs, Chillers & Cooling Distribution

+

Coolant Distribution Units (CDUs)

The CDU is the bridge between the facility cooling loop and the IT cooling loop. It contains a liquid-to-liquid heat exchanger (plate-and-frame or brazed-plate type), circulation pumps (N+1 redundancy), a fluid reservoir, filtration (5–20 micron), and control valves.

Primary (facility) side: chilled water from the chiller plant, typically 7–15°C supply, returning at 15–25°C. Secondary (IT) side: treated water or water-propylene glycol mix (20–30% glycol for freeze protection if exposed to outdoor temps), 25–45°C supply, returning at 40–60°C.

Typical CDU capacity: 250–1,400 kW per unit. One CDU per 4–8 GPU racks (row-level) or one per rack for extreme density.

Chiller Plants

Water-cooled chillers (centrifugal or screw-type compressors) with cooling towers are the standard for large GPU facilities. Coefficient of Performance (COP): 5.0–7.0 (i.e., 1 kW of compressor work removes 5–7 kW of heat). Typical chiller plant: N+1 redundancy, each chiller sized at 500–2,500 tons (1,750–8,800 kW thermal).

Air-cooled chillers (no cooling tower) have lower COP (2.5–4.0) and higher noise but simpler installation. Used in smaller deployments or where water is scarce.

Primary vs. Secondary Loops

Primary loop (facility): chiller → primary pumps → header piping → CDU primary side → return to chiller. Variable-flow with VFD-driven pumps, controlled by differential pressure setpoint. Secondary loop (IT): CDU secondary side → rack manifolds → cold plates → return to CDU. Constant or variable flow, controlled by IT inlet temperature setpoint. The two loops are hydraulically separated by the CDU’s heat exchanger, so a leak in the IT loop does not drain the facility loop (and vice versa).

Free Cooling / Economizer

When outdoor wet-bulb temperature drops below the required supply water temperature, the chiller compressors can be partially or fully bypassed. A waterside economizer uses a plate heat exchanger to transfer heat directly from the primary loop to the cooling tower loop. In climates like the US Pacific Northwest or Northern Europe, free cooling can provide 3,000–6,000+ hours/year of compressor-free operation, reducing cooling energy by 40–60%.

Direct-to-chip liquid cooling enables higher supply water temperatures (35–45°C vs. 7–12°C for CRAH coils), which dramatically expands free cooling hours — warm-water cooling at 45°C supply can free-cool year-round in most climates.

Cable Routing

+

Overhead Cable Trays

Standard for modern GPU facilities. Ladder rack or wire basket cable trays mounted at 7–9 feet above finished floor. Separate trays for: (1) high-voltage power cables (600V rated), (2) low-voltage data cables (Cat6A, fiber), (3) liquid cooling hoses. Minimum 12-inch separation between power and data trays per NEC Article 300.3 and TIA-568 standards to prevent EMI.

Fiber & Network Cabling

GPU superpods generate massive cabling volumes. A 256-GPU DGX SuperPOD requires 256 InfiniBand cables (400G OSFP connections) from DGX nodes to leaf switches, plus spine uplinks. Each DGX H100 has 8 ConnectX-7 NICs with one 400G port each, totaling 8 cables per node. Pre-terminated fiber trunk cables (MTP/MPO-16 to LC breakout) reduce install time. Cable lengths must be carefully managed: single-mode fiber for runs >100m, multimode OM4/OM5 for <100m at 400G.

Cable Management at the Rack

Vertical cable managers on both sides of each rack. Horizontal cable managers between every 1–2U of equipment. Velcro straps (never zip ties — they damage cable jackets and are single-use). Minimum bend radius: 10x cable diameter for copper, per manufacturer spec for fiber (typically 7.5–15mm for bend-insensitive single-mode). Service loops at both ends for future re-termination.

Fire Suppression

+

Clean Agent Systems

GPU datacenters use gaseous clean agent fire suppression to avoid water damage to multi-million-dollar GPU infrastructure. The two dominant agents:

  • FM-200 (HFC-227ea): Stored as a liquefied compressed gas at 360–600 PSI. Discharged in ≤10 seconds. Extinguishes by heat absorption (chemical and physical). Design concentration: 7.0–8.5% by volume. Safe for occupied spaces at design concentrations (NOAEL: 9.0%). ODP: 0, GWP: 3,220 (high — being phased down in some jurisdictions under F-gas regulations).
  • 3M Novec 1230 (FK-5-1-12): Fluoroketone fluid, stored as a liquid. Design concentration: 4.2–5.9%. ODP: 0, GWP: 1 (negligible — preferred for new installations). Atmospheric lifetime: ~5 days. Safe for occupied spaces (NOAEL: 10%). Higher cost per pound than FM-200 but increasingly specified for new builds due to environmental profile.

Detection & Activation

VESDA (Very Early Smoke Detection Apparatus) is the standard for GPU data halls. Aspirating smoke detectors continuously sample air through a pipe network, detecting smoke particles at the incipient stage (0.02–0.2% obscuration/m) — far earlier than conventional spot detectors (1–3% obscuration).

Activation sequence: VESDA Alert → VESDA Action (pre-alarm, investigation) → VESDA Fire 1 (siren, strobes, abort timer starts, 30–60 sec countdown) → VESDA Fire 2 (agent discharge, HVAC dampers close, doors seal). Double-interlock (also called cross-zone): requires two independent detectors in alarm before discharge, reducing false releases.

Why Not Water?

Pre-action sprinkler systems (dry pipe, charged only on detector activation) are used in some facilities as a secondary/backup system per code requirements (NFPA 75, NFPA 76). However, wet sprinklers are never used in GPU data halls due to the risk of catastrophic water damage. A single sprinkler head activation over a row of DGX H100 racks could destroy millions of dollars of GPU hardware. Pre-action systems provide a safety net while ensuring water only flows if both a detector activates AND a sprinkler head fuses (dual-action).

Liquid Cooling & Fire Risk

Liquid-cooled GPU facilities introduce a unique consideration: water-glycol coolant in the secondary loop. While propylene glycol is not combustible at typical concentrations (20–30%), a large leak could interact with electrical equipment. Design mitigations: leak detection auto-shutoff, drip pans under all CDUs and manifolds, sloped floors to floor drains, and GFCI/ground fault protection on all circuits in proximity to liquid cooling infrastructure.

Knowledge Check: Physical Infrastructure

Test your understanding of the concepts covered in this section. Select the best answer for each question.

Question 1: Power Redundancy

A GPU datacenter uses 2N power redundancy. Each DGX H100 node has dual PSU sets (A and B). During a routine UPS maintenance window, the A-side UPS is taken offline. What is the expected impact on the running GPU training job?

Correct: B. In a 2N configuration, each power path (A and B) is independently sized to carry 100% of the IT load. The DGX H100's PSU architecture (3+3 redundancy across 6 PSUs) ensures that either the A-set or B-set alone can power the full node. When one path is taken offline for maintenance, the other continues without any performance degradation or interruption. This is what makes Tier III (concurrently maintainable) facilities viable for GPU workloads.

Question 2: Cooling Architecture

Your facility uses direct-to-chip liquid cooling with a CDU-based architecture. The monitoring system alerts that GPU junction temperatures across an entire rack have risen from 72°C to 81°C over the past 15 minutes, approaching the 83°C throttle threshold. The CDU secondary loop supply temperature reads normal at 35°C. What is the most likely root cause?

Correct: B. The key diagnostic clue is that the CDU supply temperature is normal (35°C) but GPU temperatures are rising. If the chiller or primary loop had failed (option A), the CDU supply temperature would also have risen. Since the supply is normal, the issue is flow rate, not supply temperature. Reduced flow through the cold plates means less thermal mass to absorb GPU heat per unit time, causing junction temperatures to climb even though the entering coolant temperature is correct. Common causes include a failing secondary pump, a clogged 5-20 micron inline filter, a partially closed isolation valve, or an air pocket trapped in the highest point of the loop. SRE response: check CDU secondary pump speed/status, differential pressure across the filter, and flow meter readings.

Question 3: Fire Suppression

Your facility uses Novec 1230 clean agent fire suppression with VESDA aspirating smoke detection and a double-interlock pre-action sprinkler system as backup. A VESDA detector enters "Alert" status in Zone 3 (GPU data hall). What is the correct SRE response, and at what stage would clean agent discharge occur?

Correct: C. VESDA systems have multiple alarm levels specifically to enable graduated response. Alert is the earliest stage (0.02–0.06% obscuration/m) and may indicate dust, minor contamination, or an incipient condition — investigation is required but discharge is not imminent. Action indicates higher smoke levels; the on-call team should be responding. Fire 1 triggers audible/visual alarms and starts the abort countdown. Fire 2 (or cross-zone confirmation from a second detector) triggers actual agent discharge. The 30–60 second abort timer allows personnel to evacuate and also provides an opportunity to abort a false alarm. The double-interlock pre-action sprinklers add an additional layer: even if a clean agent discharge fails to suppress the fire, water only flows if both a detector is in alarm AND a sprinkler head physically activates from heat (>68°C / 155°F). This dual-action requirement prevents accidental water release.
