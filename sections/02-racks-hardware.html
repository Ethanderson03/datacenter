Racks & Hardware

From 42U steel to 700-watt GPUs — the physical foundation of AI supercomputing infrastructure.

1. IT Rack Standards

The 42U Rack — EIA-310-E

Nearly every piece of datacenter equipment is designed around the EIA-310-E standard (also referenced as IEC 60297 and DIN 41494). One rack unit (1U) equals 44.45 mm (1.75 in) of vertical mounting space. A standard full-height cabinet provides 42U of usable space — roughly 1,867 mm (73.5 in) of mounting height inside an overall cabinet that stands approximately 2,000–2,100 mm tall including casters and roof panels.

The 19-inch (482.6 mm) mounting width between the inner edges of the front vertical rails is the universal constant. Equipment faceplates are designed to span exactly this opening, secured with screws or tool-less clips into the rail holes.

Open Frame vs. Enclosed Cabinet

Open Frame (2-Post / 4-Post)

  • No side panels, doors, or roof — maximum airflow
  • Lower cost, lighter weight, easier cable access
  • Used in private, access-controlled datacenter rooms
  • 2-post for lightweight network and patch panels; 4-post for servers
  • Cannot form hot/cold aisle containment without additional accessories

Enclosed Cabinet

  • Locking front and rear doors, removable side panels
  • Perforated doors (typically 64–80% perforation) for front-to-back airflow
  • Required for hot-aisle/cold-aisle containment strategies
  • Physical security for colocation environments
  • Acoustic dampening in some models (noise reduction for edge deployments)

Cable Management & Airflow Best Practices

+

Vertical Cable Management

Vertical cable managers run along the sides of the rack between the mounting rails and the cabinet walls. They come in single-sided (finger) or double-sided (hinged) designs. For high-density GPU racks carrying 8–16 fiber cables and multiple power cords per server, double-sided vertical managers with at least 6″ (152 mm) of cable trough width are recommended. The NVIDIA DGX H100 alone requires 10 rear-panel cable connections (8× InfiniBand + 2× management Ethernet), making vertical management essential.

Horizontal Cable Management

1U or 2U horizontal cable managers are placed between equipment to organize cables laterally and route them to the vertical managers. In GPU-dense racks, a 1U horizontal manager should be placed above each DGX node to prevent cable congestion from blocking airflow.

Blanking Panels

Every unused rack unit must be covered with a blanking panel. Without them, hot exhaust air recirculates from the rear of the rack to the front intake through the empty openings — a phenomenon called hot-air bypass. Studies show that leaving just 4U uncovered in a 42U rack can raise intake temperatures by 5–10°C, leading to thermal throttling in GPUs. Snap-in tool-less blanking panels allow rapid reconfiguration.

Mounting Hardware

  • Cage nuts & screws: Traditional square-hole mounting. Insert cage nut with a cage-nut tool, then fasten with a M6 screw. Secure but slower to reconfigure.
  • Tool-less (clip-nut / snap-in) rails: Spring-loaded latches on server rail kits that clip into the rack’s square or round holes. Most modern 1U/2U servers ship with tool-less inner rails.
  • Sliding rail kits: Telescoping rails (ball-bearing or roller) that allow servers to slide forward for maintenance without removing cables. Required for heavy systems like the DGX H100 (approx. 60 kg / 132 lbs per 8U chassis).

Raised-floor tiles beneath GPU racks must also be rated accordingly — standard floor tiles support ~450–570 kg per tile (concentrated load), so reinforced tiles or structural support are often required.

2. NVIDIA DGX / HGX Systems

HGX vs. DGX — Understanding the Product Line

NVIDIA’s AI compute platform is delivered in two tiers:

  • HGX (Hyperscale GPU Accelerator) is a baseboard reference design — a GPU tray containing the GPU modules, NVSwitch interconnect, and NVLink wiring. NVIDIA sells HGX baseboards to OEM partners (Dell, HPE, Lenovo, Supermicro, etc.) who integrate them into their own server chassis with their own choice of CPUs, memory, storage, and network adapters. This allows customization but requires more integration effort.
  • DGX is NVIDIA’s fully integrated, turnkey AI system. It includes the HGX baseboard plus NVIDIA-selected CPUs, memory, NVMe storage, ConnectX NICs, BlueField DPUs, the chassis, cooling, firmware, and the DGX OS (based on Ubuntu Linux). DGX systems are sold directly by NVIDIA and through select partners as validated, pre-configured appliances.

Think of HGX as the engine and DGX as the complete car.

DGX B200 & GB200 NVL72 — Next Generation

+

DGX B200

The DGX B200 is the direct successor to the DGX H100, built around the NVIDIA Blackwell B200 GPU. Each B200 GPU features 208 billion transistors on a TSMC 4NP process with a dual-die design connected via a 10 TB/s chip-to-chip interconnect. Key specs per GPU:

  • 192 GB HBM3e memory at 8 TB/s bandwidth
  • Up to 9 PFLOPS FP4 Tensor performance (with sparsity)
  • NVLink 5.0: 1,800 GB/s bidirectional per GPU (2× the H100)
  • TDP: 1,000W per GPU

A DGX B200 node with 8× B200 GPUs delivers ~72 PFLOPS of FP4 AI training performance and contains 1.5 TB of HBM3e memory.

GB200 NVL72 — The Rack-Scale GPU

The GB200 NVL72 represents a fundamental shift from node-scale to rack-scale computing. A single NVL72 rack contains:

  • 36× Grace CPUs (NVIDIA’s Arm Neoverse V2-based processor, 72 cores each)
  • 72× Blackwell GPUs arranged as 36 Grace-Blackwell Superchips (each Superchip pairs 1 Grace CPU + 2 Blackwell GPUs via NVLink-C2C at 900 GB/s)
  • All 72 GPUs connected via NVLink 5.0 through 9× NVSwitch 4th gen chips, forming a single unified memory domain of ~13.5 TB HBM3e
  • Liquid-cooled design (mandatory — ~120 kW per rack)
  • ~720 PFLOPS FP4 AI performance per rack

The NVL72 is designed for trillion-parameter model training where the entire model’s weights can reside in the GPU memory of a single rack, eliminating the need for slower fabric-level communication during many training steps.

Hopper SM Architecture

Each Hopper Streaming Multiprocessor (SM) contains:

  • 128 FP32 CUDA Cores (organized as 4 processing blocks of 32 each)
  • 4 Fourth-Generation Tensor Cores — these are the workhorses for AI training and inference. Each Tensor Core can execute matrix multiply-accumulate operations on FP8, FP16, BF16, TF32, FP64, and INT8 data types.
  • 4 Texture Units
  • 256 KB Register File per SM
  • 256 KB combined L1 / Shared Memory (configurable split, up from 192 KB in Ampere A100)

The H100 SXM5 activates 132 of the 144 SMs on the GH100 die (the remaining 12 are disabled for yield improvement). This gives 132 × 128 = 16,896 CUDA cores and 132 × 4 = 528 Tensor Cores.

Transformer Engine & FP8

+

The H100 introduces the Transformer Engine, a hardware-software co-design that dynamically switches between FP8 and FP16 precision on a per-tensor, per-layer basis during training. Each Tensor Core can execute FP8 matrix operations (E4M3 or E5M2 formats) at 2× the throughput of FP16, while maintaining model accuracy through automatic scaling factors managed by the engine.

The Transformer Engine stores a per-tensor scaling factor (computed from the previous iteration) that maps the dynamic range of FP8 to the actual value distribution of each tensor. This allows gradients and activations with different dynamic ranges to each use the full precision of FP8 without overflow or underflow. Layers that are sensitive to precision automatically fall back to FP16 computation.

This is why the H100 can achieve ~4 PFLOPS FP8 per GPU (with sparsity) — double the FP16 rate — while training GPT-3-class models to the same converged accuracy as FP16 training on the A100.

NVLink 4.0 & NVSwitch 3rd Generation

NVLink 4.0 is NVIDIA’s proprietary high-bandwidth, low-latency GPU interconnect. Each H100 SXM5 GPU has 18 NVLink 4.0 links, each running at 25 GB/s per direction (50 GB/s bidirectional), for an aggregate of 900 GB/s bidirectional per GPU.

Within a DGX H100 node, the 8 GPUs are connected in an all-to-all topology via 4× NVSwitch 3rd-generation ASICs. Each NVSwitch chip has:

  • 64 NVLink 4.0 ports
  • 13.6 TB/s total aggregate switching bandwidth
  • SHARP v3 (Scalable Hierarchical Aggregation and Reduction Protocol): in-network allreduce and other collective operations executed directly in the switch silicon, reducing collective communication time by up to 2×

The 4 NVSwitch chips collectively provide every GPU with a direct, full-bandwidth path to every other GPU in the node. This means GPU 0 can read from GPU 7’s HBM3 memory at the full 900 GB/s — there is no bandwidth reduction regardless of which GPU pair is communicating. This non-blocking, all-to-all fabric is critical for large-model tensor parallelism, where layers are split across GPUs and each training step requires all GPUs to exchange activations and gradients.

DGX H100 Internal Architecture — NVSwitch All-to-All Topology

PCIe Gen5 & CPU-GPU Communication

While NVLink provides the primary high-bandwidth path between GPUs, the PCIe Gen5 bus connects each GPU to its host CPU and to its dedicated ConnectX-7 NIC. PCIe Gen5 x16 provides 128 GB/s bidirectional (64 GB/s per direction), which is sufficient for:

  • Feeding training data from NVMe storage through the CPU to GPU memory
  • Moving gradient data from GPU to ConnectX-7 NIC for inter-node allreduce over InfiniBand
  • CPU-side operations (data preprocessing, checkpoint writes)

In the DGX H100, each GPU has a dedicated PCIe Gen5 x16 connection to a ConnectX-7 NIC, ensuring GPUDirect RDMA can transfer data directly from GPU memory to the network without staging through CPU memory. This is critical for achieving line-rate 400 Gb/s InfiniBand throughput.

+

Multi-Instance GPU (MIG) allows a single H100 to be securely partitioned into up to 7 independent GPU instances. Each MIG instance has:

  • Its own dedicated set of SMs (CUDA cores and Tensor Cores)
  • Its own partition of HBM3 memory and memory bandwidth
  • Its own L2 cache partition
  • Its own video decode and JPEG engines
  • Fault isolation — an error in one instance cannot affect another

Scaling Hierarchy

SuperPOD Networking — Leaf-Spine InfiniBand Fabric

The DGX H100 SuperPOD uses a two-tier leaf-spine fat-tree topology built entirely with NVIDIA Quantum-2 QM9700 InfiniBand NDR switches (64-port, 400 Gb/s per port).

Compute Fabric

  • Leaf switches: Each QM9700 leaf switch connects to 32 DGX ports (one per NIC, across multiple DGX nodes). With 64 ports per switch, the remaining 32 ports uplink to spine switches. This gives a 1:1 subscription ratio (full bisection bandwidth).
  • Spine switches: The spine layer aggregates all leaf switches. For a 32-node SuperPOD with 256 ConnectX-7 NICs, the fabric uses 8 leaf switches and 8 spine switches (Quantum-2 QM9700), delivering a non-blocking fabric where any DGX node can communicate with any other node at the full 400 Gb/s per NIC × 8 NICs = 3.2 Tb/s per node.

Storage & Management Fabric

  • The 64 BlueField-3 DPUs (2 per node) connect to a separate InfiniBand fabric dedicated to storage traffic (connecting to NVIDIA-qualified parallel file systems like Lustre, GPFS/Spectrum Scale, or VAST Data) and in-band management.
  • An additional out-of-band Ethernet management network provides BMC/IPMI access.

DGX H100 SuperPOD — Leaf-Spine InfiniBand Fabric

Scaling Beyond a Single SuperPOD

+

For workloads requiring more than 256 GPUs, multiple SuperPODs are interconnected by adding a third tier of spine switches (sometimes called “super-spines” or “core switches”). Each SuperPOD’s existing spine switches become effectively “aggregation” switches that uplink to the super-spine layer.

NVIDIA’s Eos supercomputer (used internally for AI research) is built from 4,608 H100 GPUs (18 SuperPODs’ worth) using this multi-tier approach. The Meta AI Research SuperCluster (RSC) and Microsoft Azure’s ND H100 v5 clusters use similar architectures at even larger scale (tens of thousands of H100s).

At these scales, careful attention must be paid to:

  • Adaptive routing: InfiniBand NDR supports adaptive routing to avoid congestion hotspots in the fabric
  • Rail-optimized topology: NVIDIA’s “rail-optimized” design connects the same-numbered NIC from each DGX (e.g., NIC 3 from all 32 nodes) to the same leaf switch. This means allreduce patterns that operate per-rail see minimal switch hops.
  • Job placement: Schedulers (e.g., NVIDIA Base Command Manager) topology-aware scheduling to place multi-node jobs on nodes sharing leaf switches whenever possible
  • SHARP (Scalable Hierarchical Aggregation and Reduction Protocol): Offloads allreduce collective operations to the switch ASICs themselves, reducing network traffic by up to 2× for data-parallel training

5. NVIDIA BlueField DPU

What is a DPU (Data Processing Unit)?

A Data Processing Unit (DPU) is a specialized processor that combines a high-performance network interface with a programmable multi-core CPU and hardware acceleration engines. It sits on the PCIe bus alongside GPUs and CPUs but handles infrastructure-level tasks that would otherwise consume host CPU cycles or require dedicated appliances:

  • Network virtualization: VXLAN/Geneve encap/decap, OVS offload, connection tracking
  • Storage disaggregation: NVMe-oF (NVMe over Fabrics) target and initiator, compression, encryption
  • Security: IPsec/TLS inline encryption, hardware root of trust, secure boot chain
  • Telemetry: Per-flow monitoring, congestion detection, What Just Happened (WJH) analytics

In a DGX H100, the 2 BlueField-3 DPUs handle all storage I/O and management-plane networking, freeing the host CPUs and GPUs to focus exclusively on AI computation. The DPU runs its own ARM-based operating system (typically Ubuntu on the ARM cores) and presents virtualized devices to the host.

DOCA SDK & BlueField Programming Model

+

DOCA (Data Center Infrastructure on a Chip Architecture) is NVIDIA’s SDK for programming BlueField DPUs. It provides:

  • DOCA Flow: Hardware-offloaded packet processing pipeline (match + action, similar to P4 or OpenFlow concepts). Programs the DPU’s eSwitch hardware to process packets at line rate without involving the ARM cores.
  • DOCA GPUNetIO: Enables GPUDirect RDMA from GPU memory through the DPU, allowing GPU-to-storage data paths that bypass the host CPU entirely.
  • DOCA Compress / Decompress: Hardware-accelerated LZ4 and Deflate for inline data reduction on storage traffic.
  • DOCA SHA / AES / Regex: Cryptographic and pattern-matching acceleration for security workloads.
  • DOCA App Shield: Runtime integrity monitoring of the host OS and applications from the DPU’s isolated security domain.

Developers write DOCA applications in C, which run on the BlueField’s ARM cores. The DOCA libraries abstract the DPU’s hardware accelerators, allowing developers to express intent (e.g., “encrypt this flow at line rate”) without managing register-level hardware details. DOCA also provides a gRPC-based orchestration layer so that a central controller can configure DPU policies across hundreds of nodes.

In the DGX SuperPOD context, the BlueField-3 DPUs are primarily used for:

  1. GPUDirect Storage: Moving training data from the parallel file system directly to GPU memory at 200 Gb/s per DPU, bypassing host CPU and system memory. This is essential for keeping 8 H100 GPUs fed with data during training.
  2. In-band management: Running NVIDIA Base Command Manager agents on the DPU’s ARM cores for health monitoring, telemetry collection, and firmware updates without impacting the host OS.
  3. Network isolation: Providing tenant-level isolation in multi-user environments by virtualizing the storage network through the DPU’s hardware SR-IOV and virtio capabilities.

Knowledge Check

Question 1: NVSwitch Topology

In a DGX H100 node, how are the 8 H100 GPUs interconnected via NVSwitch?

Correct: C. The DGX H100 uses 4 NVSwitch 3rd-gen chips to create a non-blocking all-to-all topology. Each of the 8 GPUs connects to all 4 NVSwitch chips. The switches act as crossbars, routing traffic so that any GPU pair communicates at the full 900 GB/s bidirectional NVLink bandwidth. This is not a direct mesh (which would require 28 separate point-to-point links) — the NVSwitch chips provide the switching fabric that makes the all-to-all connectivity feasible in the 8-GPU package.

Question 2: DGX SuperPOD Scale

A DGX H100 SuperPOD consists of 32 DGX H100 nodes. What is the total number of ConnectX-7 NICs used for the compute InfiniBand fabric, and why is this number significant?

Correct: B. Each DGX H100 contains 8 ConnectX-7 NICs — one per GPU — connected via PCIe Gen5 x16. This 1:1 GPU-to-NIC ratio ensures that each GPU can perform GPUDirect RDMA over InfiniBand at 400 Gb/s independently and simultaneously, giving each node 8 × 400 Gb/s = 3.2 Tb/s of aggregate fabric bandwidth. Across 32 nodes, that is 256 NICs total. The 64 BlueField-3 DPUs (2 per node) are on a separate storage/management fabric and are not counted in the compute NIC total.

Question 3: BlueField DPU Role

What is the primary reason the DGX H100 includes 2 BlueField-3 DPUs in addition to its 8 ConnectX-7 NICs?

Correct: D. BlueField-3 DPUs are infrastructure processors, not compute accelerators. They run their own ARM-based OS and handle storage traffic (via NVMe-oF / GPUDirect Storage), in-band management (NVIDIA Base Command Manager), security (hardware IPsec/TLS), and telemetry. This separation ensures that the host Xeon CPUs are not burdened with storage and management overhead during AI training, where CPU cycles are needed for data loading and preprocessing. The DPUs connect to a separate InfiniBand fabric dedicated to storage, distinct from the compute fabric used by the ConnectX-7 NICs.
